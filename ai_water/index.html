How Data Centers Consume Water

MECHANISM
PRIMARY METHOD — MOST COMMON IN NEBRASKA
Evaporative Cooling (Open-Loop)
Hot server air or water is cooled by passing it through a cooling tower where water evaporates, carrying heat into the atmosphere. The evaporated water is gone permanently — it never returns to the local aquifer or river system. This is the dominant method at Nebraska's hyperscale campuses because it uses less electricity, and Nebraska's moderate climate makes it efficient.

⚠ ~80% of water withdrawn evaporates and is permanently consumed.
EMERGING — REDUCES WATER FOOTPRINT
Direct-to-Chip Liquid Cooling (Closed-Loop)
Liquid coolant is circulated directly through pipes attached to CPU and GPU chips. Heat is removed mechanically without evaporation, dramatically reducing water consumption — by up to 95% compared to open-loop systems. Meta's newest facilities use a closed-loop approach and report a WUE of 0.20 L/kWh vs. the 1.8 L/kWh industry average. AI-optimized racks increasingly require this method due to extreme chip heat density.

✓ Eliminates evaporative loss; uses more electricity instead.

Ogallala Aquifer — Nebraska Geography

RISK ZONES
LOW DIRECT RISK — CURRENT DATA CENTER ZONE
Eastern Nebraska (Sarpy / Lancaster / Douglas)
This is where all current data centers sit. The Ogallala does not extend into this area — instead, eastern Nebraska draws from the shallow alluvial aquifer connected to the Platte and Missouri Rivers, which recharges relatively quickly from precipitation and river interaction. Data centers here strain municipal water systems rather than directly drawing from the Ogallala.
+0% aquifer decline since 1950 in eastern NE (some areas have risen)
TRANSITION ZONE — MONITOR CLOSELY
Central Nebraska (York / Fillmore / Butler Counties)
This area sits at the eastern fringe of the Ogallala. UNL's 2024 groundwater report found the largest year-over-year declines here — 10 to 15 feet lower than 2023 due to drought. If data center expansion moves westward along I-80, this zone becomes a flashpoint. Already stressed by irrigation agriculture, it has limited capacity to absorb large industrial water draws.
-15 ft water level decline 2023→2024 (UNL annual report)
HIGH RISK — AQUIFER CRITICALLY STRESSED
Southwest Nebraska (Chase / Dundy / Hayes Counties)
The southwestern corner of Nebraska mirrors conditions seen in Kansas and the Texas Panhandle. Water levels have fallen more than 100 feet since the 1950s in Chase County. The Ogallala here is thin and recharges at a glacially slow rate — some estimates suggest natural recharge would take hundreds to thousands of years. No data centers currently, but any expansion into this corridor would be highly concerning.
-100 ft water level decline since 1950 in Chase County
ABUNDANT BUT MONITOR
West-Central Nebraska (Sandhills region)
The Nebraska Sandhills sit over the deepest and most voluminous section of the entire Ogallala — saturated thickness exceeds 1,000 feet in some areas, and Nebraska holds roughly 70% of all Ogallala water. The Sandhills' sandy soils also allow relatively high recharge rates. This zone is currently healthy, but it directly overlies the aquifer and any industrial-scale pumping would face strong NRD scrutiny and public opposition.
1,000+ ft saturated thickness — world's largest freshwater deposit

CRITICAL MECHANISM — THE MUNICIPAL WATER CHAIN
Why "not directly on the Ogallala" still matters
Data centers in eastern Nebraska draw roughly 95% of their water from municipal water utilities — not their own wells. This sounds reassuring, but it obscures the full chain: Papillion's municipal water system primarily draws from the alluvial aquifer connected to the Platte River. That alluvial system is hydrologically connected to the Ogallala. When cities pump more to supply data centers, the drawdown propagates. In Minnesota, data center activity has already been linked to measurable groundwater level drops. In Newton County, Georgia, Meta's facility was permitted to use over 500,000 gallons per day — residents reported lower water pressure and supply issues within a year. Nebraska is earlier in this trajectory, but the underlying mechanism is identical.

Water Research: FOIA & Records Request Targets

ACTION ITEMS
Papio-Missouri River NRD
COVERS SARPY & DOUGLAS COUNTIES
The NRD that covers the data center hotspot. Request all industrial well permits (50+ gpm threshold) issued since 2015 within zip codes 68046, 68138, 68133 (Papillion/Bellevue area). Also request any large-volume water allocation agreements.
REQUEST: "All industrial or commercial groundwater well permits issued in Sarpy and Douglas counties from 2015–present with permitted capacity exceeding 50 gallons per minute, including owner name, permitted volume, and GPS coordinates."
Lower Platte South NRD
COVERS LANCASTER COUNTY (LINCOLN)
Governs groundwater in the Lincoln area where Google's "Project Agate" is under construction. Request industrial water permits and any variance applications associated with large-scale developments since 2021 in SE Lancaster County.
REQUEST: "All industrial groundwater well permits and surface water diversion permits issued in Lancaster County since 2021 for facilities with projected water consumption exceeding 100,000 gallons per day."
City of Papillion Utilities
MUNICIPAL WATER RECORDS
City utilities track water sold to industrial customers. Data center companies are the largest industrial water users in the city and should appear prominently. Monthly consumption data by account type is typically public record.
REQUEST: "Annual water consumption records by customer class (industrial/commercial) for 2015–present, including the top 10 water users by annual volume and their addresses."
ImagiNE Nebraska Applications
NE DEPT. OF ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT
Companies applying for data center tax incentives must submit detailed project plans including estimated water consumption. These applications may contain facility-level water projections that companies don't voluntarily disclose publicly.
REQUEST: "All ImagiNE Nebraska Act applications from 2019–present for data center or technology infrastructure projects, including projected annual water consumption figures submitted as part of the application."
UNL Conservation & Survey Division
GROUNDWATER MONITORING DATA
UNL monitors ~5,000 wells across Nebraska annually. Request well-level monitoring data for the Platte River alluvial zone in Sarpy, Douglas, and Lancaster counties — specifically trend data from 2017 to present to see if data center construction correlates with level changes.
REQUEST: "Annual groundwater level measurements from 2015–2024 for monitoring wells within 10 miles of Papillion, NE (zip 68046) and Lincoln, NE (zip 68521), including well IDs, GPS coordinates, and measured depths."
Nebraska DWEE (Dept. Water, Energy & Environment)
SURFACE WATER PERMITS
Formed July 2025 from the merger of DNR and NDEE. Holds all surface water diversion permit records. Search for industrial water rights granted to shell LLCs near known data center locations. Their online portal allows owner-name searches.
REQUEST: "All surface water diversion permits issued since 2015 to holders in Sarpy, Lancaster, and Douglas counties for industrial or cooling purposes, including permitted volumes and source waterway."
